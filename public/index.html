Covid-19 infections scrollyteller

Tue 17 Mar 2020, 5:21 PM AEDT

This is every case of COVID 19 around the world. There’s a lot going on, and it’s very hard to try to understand what it even means. Well we’re here to help - this piece is going to show you how to read these charts, and what each country is doing, the successful ones and the ones like italy currently being overwhelmed.

Before we do this, there is one concept that is vitally important to understanding a pandemic - exponential growth. What sounds like small differences in the rate of spread of the virus have huge impacts in how many people are infected. Let's show you how.

The way to measure the growth of COVID 19 is how long it takes for the amount of cases in a country to double. Here are three different scenarios - doubling every two days, every three days, and every week.

Initially the differences are small.

But as the numbers grow the differences increase dramatically.

Within a month the differences are stark - and remember this example started from one case. In Australia there are currenly XX cases.

It’s for this reason that early intervention is so much more effective.

OK, so what does that look like in the real world, well let's go to the source of the outbreak- China.

As you can see, in China the virus initially spread at a fast rate, but it now appears the country has reduced the spread - how did they do that? We’ll cover that further down.

So what does that mean for the rest of the world - lets bring everyone back in.

That’s not really helpful, is there a way to be able to compare countries with small outbreaks like Australia with China.

Firstly we’re going to narrow it down to a few key countries that will help you understand what we’re up against - China, Australia, Italy, South Korea, Singapore, United Kingdom, United States.

We’re also going to change it so we can compare the shape of each outbreak more easily, so instead of showing you the date, we’re going to start it from when each country has 100 cases.

It’s still hard to compare though, and the important bit is the shape of the spread, so we’re going to use a logarithmic scale…. A what! What that means is is that the vertical axis is scaled, so instead of normal numbers, it is scaled by powers of 10.

You’ll see that those trend lines are now straight - and that’s why this sort of chart is good for comparing outbreaks.

So now we can compare countries, especially at the important time when the virus first takes off.

So what we want to do as much as possible is stick to a lower trend line.

This is known as trying to flatten the curve, because outbreaks follow a curve.

More and more people are infected until enough of the population has been infected that those new people who catch the virus don’t pass it on.

So if everyone is going to be infected, why don’t we just let the virus run through the population. There are a few important reasons, firstly we want to keep infections at a rate where the health system can handle those people who get really sick.

This reduces the risk of health services being overwhelmed like what we’re seeing in Italy.

In this case COVID 19 seems to be impacting older people, and those with chronic health conditions. If the health system can’t treat those people properly then we know this will increase the amount of people dying.

Secondly while we can’t stop the virus from spreading through the populations, we do have some control over how many people will get infected overall. By reducing that number we can reduce the number of those vulnerable people who are at risk of dying.

OK so how do you flatten that curve?!?
